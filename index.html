How does the distribution of function in sequence space shape evolutionary outcomes?

Epistasis (non-additivity between mutations) is a critical factor shaping evolution

The case of oseltamivir resistance in H1N1 influenza

  • In 1991, the H274Y mutation to an influenza protein was shown to compromise viral fitness
  • In 2008, H274Y entered the H1N1 population and conferred resistance to the drug oseltamivir
  • Between 1991 and 2008, mutations accumulated that offset the deleterious effect of H274Y.
  • Epistasis between these mutations opened a new evolutionary path

Bloom et al. (2010) Science 328:1272

Epistasis can have many effects:

  • Limits evolutionary trajectories (making evolution follow predictable trajectories).
    Weinreich et al (2006) Science 312(7):111
  • Stochastically opens evolutionary trajectories (making evolution contingent on the past).
    Harms & Thornton (2014) Nature 512:203
  • "Entrenches" evolutionary trajectories (making evolution irreversible).
    Shah et al. (2015) PNAS 112(25):E3226

Study high-order epistasis with simple protein computational model

  • Pairwise-only (second order) interactions
  • Can exhaustively sample thermodynamic ensembles across sequence space
Implementation from: Bloom et al. (2006). PNAS 103(15):5869-5874

Strategy

  1. Generate a 6-site ($2^{6} = 64$ protein) sequence space
  2. Generate full thermodynamic ensembles for each sequence
  3. Calculate $\Delta G_{native}$ for each ensemble
  4. Fit the high-order epistasis model to these data

Lattice model sequence space exhibits high-order epistasis

Where does this come from in a simple pairwise model?

$$\Delta G_{A} = E_{A} - RTln \Big (Q - e^{-E_{A}/RT} \Big )$$

For an ensemble with only states $A$ and $B$:

$$\Delta G_{A} = E_{A} - E_{B}$$

A two-state model should not exhibit high-order epistasis

Two-state lattice model does not exhibit high-order epistasis

If we add a third state, we expect high-order epistasis

$$\Delta G_{A} = E_{A} - RTln \Big (e^{-E_{B}/RT} + e^{-E_{C}/RT} \Big )$$

Three-state simulation: high-order epistasis

High-order epistasis arises from nonlinearity in system

  • Each mutation affects more than one conformation
  • The system thus responds nonlinearly to mutations.
  • The linear model accounts for this with high-order terms

Where does it come from?

  • From nonlinearity in the genotype-phenotype map

Conclusions:

  • Biological phenotypes are generally non-linear
  • Genotype space is fundamentally linear
  • This mismatch means that high-order epistasis is "baked" into sequence space
  • High-order epistasis leads to a profound "memory" in evolution: the effect of a mutation depends on all mutations that preceded it.
  • Historical contingency, entrenchment, irreversibility, locally predictable evolutionary paths are the rule.
